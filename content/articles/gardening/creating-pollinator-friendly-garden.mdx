## Why Pollinators Need Your Help

Pollinators are in trouble. Bee populations have declined by nearly 30 percent in recent decades, and many butterfly species are at historic lows. Habitat loss, pesticide use, disease, and climate change have combined to create a crisis that threatens not just these fascinating creatures but the entire food system that depends on them.

One-third of the food we eat depends on pollinator activity. Without bees, butterflies, hummingbirds, and other pollinators, we would lose most fruits, many vegetables, nuts, and the flowering plants that beautify our world.

The encouraging news is that home gardens can make a real difference. Even a small garden designed with pollinators in mind provides food, shelter, and breeding habitat that helps sustain local pollinator populations. Collectively, residential gardens represent millions of acres of potential habitat.

## Understanding What Pollinators Need

All pollinators have three basic needs: food sources (nectar and pollen), shelter and nesting sites, and water. A truly pollinator-friendly garden addresses all three.

**Food comes from flowers.** Different pollinators are active at different times of the year, so providing flowers from early spring through late fall ensures a continuous food supply. Diversity is key — a wide variety of flower shapes, colors, and bloom times supports the widest range of pollinator species.

**Shelter** includes overwintering sites, nesting areas, and protection from weather and predators. Ground-nesting bees need patches of bare, undisturbed soil. Cavity-nesting bees use hollow stems, holes in wood, and pre-made bee houses. Butterflies need dense shrubs and foliage for roosting.

**Water** is essential but often overlooked. A shallow dish with pebbles and fresh water provides a safe drinking station for butterflies and bees. The pebbles give insects a place to land without risk of drowning.

## Choosing the Best Plants

### Native Plants Are Always the Best Choice

Native plants and native pollinators have co-evolved over thousands of years. Native bees are specifically adapted to extract pollen from native flower shapes, and native butterflies often depend on specific native host plants for their caterpillars. Research which plants are native to your specific region and prioritize those in your garden.

### Top Pollinator Plants by Season

**Early Spring Bloomers:** Crocus, hellebore, pussy willow, native plum trees, and early-blooming wildflowers provide crucial food when pollinators emerge from dormancy and few other food sources are available.

**Late Spring:** Columbine, wild geranium, lupine, rhododendron, and fruit tree blossoms. This is when many bee colonies are building up their populations and need abundant food.

**Summer:** Coneflower (Echinacea), black-eyed Susan, bee balm (Monarda), lavender, sunflowers, milkweed, and zinnias. Summer provides the longest bloom period and the most diverse pollinator activity.

**Late Summer and Fall:** Goldenrod, asters, sedum, Joe Pye weed, and fall-blooming anemones. These plants are critical for pollinators building fat reserves for winter or preparing for migration, as monarch butterflies do.

### Plants Butterflies Love

Butterflies are attracted to flat, open flowers where they can land and feed easily. Top butterfly-attracting plants include butterfly bush (Buddleja), lantana, verbena, phlox, and pentas. Plant these in sunny, sheltered areas where butterflies can bask.

Equally important are host plants where butterflies lay their eggs and caterpillars feed. Monarchs require milkweed. Swallowtails use parsley, dill, and fennel. Painted ladies feed on thistle and hollyhock. Without these specific host plants, butterfly populations cannot reproduce in your area.

### Plants Bees Prefer

Bees are attracted to blue, purple, yellow, and white flowers. They prefer tubular flowers like salvia, foxglove, and penstemon, as well as composite flowers like daisies and asters. Bees also love herb flowers — let some of your basil, oregano, thyme, and mint bolt and flower for a bee feast.

Single-flowered varieties are far better for bees than double-flowered cultivars. Double flowers have extra petals that often block access to nectar and pollen, making them beautiful to humans but useless to bees.

## Garden Design for Pollinators

### Plant in Drifts, Not Singles

Pollinators are more likely to find and visit your garden when flowers are planted in groups or drifts rather than scattered as individual plants. Plant at least three to five plants of each species together. Larger masses of color are visible from greater distances and provide more efficient foraging.

### Create Layers

Design your garden with varying heights: tall plants in back, medium in the middle, and low-growing plants in front. This layered approach maximizes the number of flowers in a given space and provides diverse habitat types. Trees and shrubs form the canopy layer, perennials create the middle layer, and groundcovers fill the lowest level.

### Include a Variety of Flower Shapes

Different pollinators have different feeding equipment. Long-tongued bees and butterflies can access tubular flowers, while short-tongued bees prefer open, daisy-like flowers. Including a variety of flower shapes ensures that all pollinator types can find food.

### Provide Sunny, Sheltered Spots

Most pollinators prefer warm, sunny conditions. Plant your pollinator garden in a location that receives at least six hours of direct sun. Include a windbreak on the prevailing wind side using hedges or fencing. Butterflies particularly benefit from sheltered, sunny spots where they can warm their wings.

## Creating Habitat Features

### Bee Nesting Sites

Leave patches of bare, sandy, well-drained soil for ground-nesting bees, which include 70 percent of native bee species. Resist the urge to mulch every inch of your garden. A few unmulched, undisturbed areas in sunny spots provide essential nesting habitat.

Install a bee house for mason bees and leafcutter bees. These are simple structures with tubes or drilled holes (5/16 inch diameter, 6 inches deep) that mimic the natural cavities these bees nest in. Mount the bee house at eye level facing south or southeast.

### Leave the Leaves

In fall, resist the urge to clean up every last leaf. Many beneficial insects overwinter in leaf litter, hollow plant stems, and fallen debris. Leave a portion of your garden undisturbed through winter. Cut back perennial stems in spring rather than fall to allow overwintering insects to emerge.

### Avoid Pesticides

Pesticides, including organic options, kill pollinators along with pest insects. Practice integrated pest management by tolerating minor pest damage, encouraging natural predators like ladybugs and lacewings, and using targeted treatments only when absolutely necessary.

If you must use a pesticide, apply it in the evening when pollinators are less active, choose the least toxic option available, and never spray open flowers.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Will a pollinator garden attract too many bees and become dangerous?**
No. Bees visiting flowers are focused on foraging and are not aggressive. Unlike yellowjackets (which are wasps, not bees), honeybees and native bees rarely sting unless directly threatened. Gardens with active pollinators are safe for children and pets.

**How big does a pollinator garden need to be?**
Even a single container of flowering herbs on a balcony provides food for pollinators. Larger gardens support more species, but every flower counts.

**Can I create a pollinator garden in shade?**
Most pollinator plants prefer full sun, but shade-tolerant options exist. Wild ginger, astilbe, bleeding heart, and native woodland wildflowers provide food for shade-visiting pollinators.

Creating a pollinator-friendly garden is one of the most meaningful contributions you can make to the environment. Your garden becomes a vital link in a network of habitat that supports the pollinators we all depend on, and in return, your plants will be better pollinated, more productive, and more beautiful than ever.
