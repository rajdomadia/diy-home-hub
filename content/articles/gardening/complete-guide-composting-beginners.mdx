## What Is Composting and Why Does It Matter

Composting is the natural process of breaking down organic materials into a dark, crumbly, earthy-smelling substance called humus. This substance is essentially nature's perfect soil amendment — rich in nutrients, teeming with beneficial microorganisms, and capable of transforming even the poorest soil into a productive growing medium.

On a practical level, composting reduces the amount of waste your household sends to landfills by 30 to 40 percent. Food scraps and yard waste in landfills produce methane, a potent greenhouse gas. Composting these materials at home converts them into a resource instead of a pollution source.

For gardeners, compost is irreplaceable. It improves soil structure in both clay and sandy soils, increases water retention, provides slow-release nutrition for plants, and supports the complex ecosystem of beneficial bacteria, fungi, and organisms that healthy plants depend on.

## Understanding the Science

Composting is driven by microorganisms — bacteria, fungi, and actinomycetes — that consume organic material and break it down into simpler compounds. These organisms need four things to thrive: carbon-rich materials (browns), nitrogen-rich materials (greens), water, and oxygen.

**Carbon materials (browns)** provide energy for the composting organisms and give the compost its structure. Browns include dried leaves, cardboard, newspaper, straw, wood chips, sawdust, and dried plant stalks. They tend to be dry, brown, and woody.

**Nitrogen materials (greens)** provide protein and amino acids that the organisms need to grow and reproduce. Greens include kitchen fruit and vegetable scraps, fresh grass clippings, coffee grounds, tea bags, fresh plant trimmings, and manure from herbivorous animals.

The ideal carbon-to-nitrogen ratio for active composting is approximately 25 to 30 parts carbon to 1 part nitrogen by weight. In practical terms, this translates to roughly three parts browns to one part greens by volume. Getting this ratio approximately right is more important than being precise.

**Water** keeps the organisms alive and facilitates the chemical reactions of decomposition. Your compost pile should be as moist as a wrung-out sponge — damp throughout but not dripping wet.

**Oxygen** is needed by aerobic decomposition organisms, which are the most efficient composters and produce compost without unpleasant odors. Turning or aerating your compost pile introduces oxygen and keeps these aerobic organisms active.

## Choosing a Composting Method

### Open Pile or Bin Composting

The simplest approach is a freestanding pile or an enclosed bin in your yard. Pile composting requires a minimum size of roughly 3x3x3 feet to generate enough heat for efficient decomposition.

Compost bins contain the pile neatly, retain heat and moisture, and discourage animals. Options range from simple wire mesh enclosures to commercial tumbler bins to DIY pallet bins. A three-bin system allows you to have one bin actively filling, one cooking, and one holding finished compost.

### Tumbler Composting

Compost tumblers are sealed, rotating drums that make turning easy — simply spin the tumbler. They produce compost faster than static piles (often in four to eight weeks) because the frequent turning provides excellent aeration. They also deter animals and keep the composting area tidy.

The downside is limited capacity. Most tumblers hold three to ten cubic feet of material, which fills up quickly if you generate significant yard waste.

### Vermicomposting (Worm Composting)

Vermicomposting uses red wiggler worms to process food scraps in a contained bin. It is ideal for apartment dwellers or anyone without yard space for traditional composting. A worm bin can be kept indoors, in a garage, or on a balcony.

Red wiggler worms consume their own body weight in food scraps daily. A starting colony of one pound of worms (approximately 1,000 worms) can process three to four pounds of food waste per week.

Worm castings (the finished product) are an exceptionally rich fertilizer and soil amendment, often called black gold by gardeners.

## What to Compost

**Yes — compost these items:**
- Fruit and vegetable scraps, peels, and cores
- Coffee grounds and paper filters
- Tea bags (remove any staples)
- Eggshells (crushed for faster breakdown)
- Bread and grains (in moderation)
- Nutshells (except walnut shells, which contain juglone)
- Dried leaves and yard trimmings
- Grass clippings (mix with browns to prevent matting)
- Cardboard and newspaper (shredded, non-glossy)
- Wood ash (sparingly — it raises pH)
- Hair and nail clippings
- Cotton and wool fabric scraps (natural fibers only)
- Houseplant trimmings

**No — do not compost these items:**
- Meat, fish, bones, or dairy products (attract pests and create odors)
- Oils, fats, and grease
- Pet waste from dogs or cats (may contain harmful pathogens)
- Diseased plant material (diseases can survive composting)
- Weeds that have gone to seed (seeds may survive)
- Treated or painted wood
- Charcoal ash (contains chemicals harmful to plants)
- Synthetic materials, plastic, or glass

## Building Your First Compost Pile

### Step 1: Choose Your Location

Place your compost bin or pile in a convenient but unobtrusive spot in your yard. Partial shade is ideal — full sun dries the pile out quickly, while deep shade slows decomposition. Ensure good drainage beneath the pile so excess water does not pool.

### Step 2: Start with a Brown Layer

Lay down a six-inch base layer of coarse brown materials like small branches, straw, or dry leaves. This base layer promotes air circulation from the bottom of the pile.

### Step 3: Alternate Green and Brown Layers

Add a two to three-inch layer of green materials (kitchen scraps, fresh grass clippings). Cover with a four to six-inch layer of brown materials. Continue alternating layers as you accumulate materials.

The brown covering layer is important — it controls odors, discourages flies, and balances the moisture from wet green materials.

### Step 4: Maintain Moisture

After adding each set of layers, water lightly if the materials are dry. The pile should feel moist throughout. If you squeeze a handful of compost and a few drops of water come out, the moisture level is about right.

### Step 5: Turn Regularly

Turn or mix your compost pile every one to two weeks using a garden fork or compost aerating tool. Turning introduces oxygen, distributes moisture evenly, and moves material from the cool edges to the hot center.

A well-maintained compost pile reaches internal temperatures of 130 to 160 degrees Fahrenheit during active decomposition. This heat kills weed seeds and pathogens. If your pile is not heating up, it may need more nitrogen (greens), more water, or better aeration.

## Knowing When Compost Is Ready

Finished compost is dark brown to black, crumbly, and has an earthy smell like forest soil. You should not be able to identify any of the original materials. The pile will have shrunk to about one-third of its original volume.

Compost is typically ready in two to six months depending on the method, materials, climate, and how actively you manage the pile. Hot-managed piles with regular turning finish fastest.

Test your compost by placing a handful in a sealed bag for a few days. If it smells earthy and pleasant when you open the bag, it is ready. If it smells sour or like ammonia, it needs more time.

## Using Finished Compost

**As a soil amendment:** Mix two to four inches of compost into garden beds before planting. It improves soil structure, adds nutrients, and increases beneficial microbial activity.

**As mulch:** Spread a one to two-inch layer around plants to retain moisture, suppress weeds, and slowly feed the soil.

**As potting mix component:** Mix compost with perlite and peat moss or coconut coir to create nutrient-rich potting soil for containers.

**As compost tea:** Steep a bag of compost in water for 24 to 48 hours to create a liquid fertilizer that can be applied to plant foliage or poured around roots.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does composting attract rats or other pests?**
Properly managed compost with a balanced ratio and brown covering layer does not attract pests. Avoid adding meat, dairy, or oily foods, and keep the pile covered.

**Can I compost in winter?**
Yes, though decomposition slows significantly in cold temperatures. Continue adding materials throughout winter. The pile will reactivate as temperatures rise in spring.

**How long does it take to get usable compost?**
With active management (regular turning, proper ratios), compost can be ready in two to three months. Passive piles that are rarely turned may take six months to a year.

Composting is one of the most impactful environmental actions you can take at home. It closes the loop between consumption and production, turning waste into wealth for your garden while reducing your household's contribution to landfills.
