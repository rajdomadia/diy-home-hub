## Why You Should Fix That Drip Today

A leaky faucet is more than just an annoyance. A faucet dripping at one drop per second wastes over 3,000 gallons of water per year. That is the equivalent of more than 180 showers. Beyond the waste, a persistent drip increases your water bill, can leave mineral stains in your sink, and may indicate a worsening problem that could lead to more expensive repairs if ignored.

The good news is that most faucet leaks are straightforward to fix. The repair typically requires basic tools, inexpensive replacement parts, and about 30 minutes of your time. Once you understand the type of faucet you have, the fix becomes a simple parts swap.

## Identify Your Faucet Type

All residential faucets fall into one of four categories. Identifying your type determines which repair approach to follow.

**Compression faucets** have two handles that you tighten to stop the flow. They are the oldest and simplest design, common in older homes. These faucets use rubber washers that compress against a valve seat to stop water flow.

**Ball faucets** have a single handle that moves over a rounded ball-shaped cap directly above the spout base. The ball inside has chambers and spring-loaded rubber seals that control water flow and temperature.

**Cartridge faucets** can have one or two handles. They use a movable cartridge that slides up and down or rotates to control flow. Single-handle cartridge faucets move up for on/off and left/right for temperature.

**Ceramic disc faucets** are the most modern type and use two ceramic discs that slide over each other to control flow. They typically have a single lever and a wide cylindrical body beneath the handle.

## Tools and Materials

For most faucet repairs, you will need the following basic tools. The specific replacement parts depend on your faucet type, which we will cover in each section.

- Adjustable wrench
- Phillips and flathead screwdrivers
- Allen wrench set (hex keys)
- Needle-nose pliers
- Plumber's grease
- Replacement parts specific to your faucet (washers, O-rings, cartridge, or seals)
- Cloth or towel to protect the sink surface

## Before You Start

Turn off the water supply valves beneath the sink. There should be two valves — one for hot and one for cold. Turn both clockwise until they stop. Open the faucet handle to release any remaining water pressure in the lines.

Place a towel or cloth in the sink basin to prevent small parts from falling down the drain. Close the drain stopper as an additional safeguard.

Have a phone or camera handy to photograph each step during disassembly. This reference is invaluable when reassembling, especially for faucets with multiple small components.

## Fixing a Compression Faucet

Compression faucets almost always leak due to worn rubber washers. Here is how to replace them.

Remove the decorative cap from the handle top (pry gently with a flathead screwdriver). Unscrew the handle screw beneath it and pull the handle off. Use an adjustable wrench to unscrew the packing nut, then pull out the stem assembly.

At the bottom of the stem, you will find a rubber washer held in place by a brass screw. Remove the screw and the old washer. Take the old washer to a hardware store to find an exact size match. Install the new washer and brass screw. Coat the washer with plumber's grease.

While you have the stem removed, inspect the valve seat inside the faucet body. If it feels rough or pitted, use a seat wrench to remove it and replace it, or use a seat grinder tool to smooth the surface. A damaged seat will chew through new washers quickly.

Reassemble in reverse order. Tighten the packing nut snugly but not excessively — overtightening makes the handle difficult to turn.

## Fixing a Ball Faucet

Ball faucets have many small parts, and leaks can originate from several locations. The easiest approach is to buy a ball faucet repair kit that includes all replaceable components.

Remove the handle by loosening the Allen screw on the side. Use the special wrench included in the repair kit to remove the cap and collar. Lift out the cam, cam washer, and the rotating ball.

Inside the faucet body, you will find spring-loaded rubber seals in the inlet holes. Use needle-nose pliers to remove the old springs and seals. Install the new springs and seals from your repair kit, pressing them firmly into the inlet holes.

Replace the ball if the repair kit includes a new one. Reinstall the cam washer and cam, aligning the tab on the cam with the notch in the faucet body. Replace the cap, collar, and handle.

Also replace the O-rings on the faucet body if the leak was coming from the base of the spout. Roll the old O-rings off, coat the new ones with plumber's grease, and roll them into the grooves.

## Fixing a Cartridge Faucet

Cartridge faucet leaks are almost always solved by replacing the cartridge itself. Remove the handle, then use pliers to pull the retaining clip that holds the cartridge in place. Grip the cartridge stem with pliers and pull it straight out.

Take the old cartridge to a hardware store to match the replacement. Cartridges are brand and model-specific, so an exact match is important. Note the orientation of the old cartridge — the new one must be installed in the same position.

Push the new cartridge into the housing, ensuring it is fully seated. Replace the retaining clip, handle, and test.

## Fixing a Ceramic Disc Faucet

Remove the handle and unscrew the escutcheon cap to access the disc cartridge. Unscrew the mounting screws that hold the cartridge in the faucet body. Lift out the cartridge.

On the bottom of the cartridge, you will find rubber seals in the inlet and outlet ports. Pry these out with a small screwdriver and inspect them. If they are cracked, hardened, or deformed, replace them. Ceramic disc seal kits are available for most major brands.

Clean the seal seats on the cartridge and the corresponding surfaces in the faucet body. Any debris here will prevent a proper seal and cause the leak to return.

If the ceramic discs themselves are cracked or scored, replace the entire cartridge. Ceramic discs rarely fail, but hard water deposits can eventually damage them.

Reassemble the faucet and turn the water back on slowly. With ceramic disc faucets, always turn the water on gradually to avoid cracking the discs with a sudden pressure surge.

## When to Call a Professional

While most faucet repairs are DIY-friendly, some situations warrant professional help. Call a plumber if you find corroded supply lines or valves beneath the sink, if the shutoff valves will not turn or do not fully stop the water, if you discover water damage in the cabinet beneath the sink, or if the faucet body itself is cracked or corroded.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I know which replacement parts to buy?**
Note your faucet brand and model number, which is usually on a sticker or plate under the sink or on the faucet body. Take the old part to the hardware store for a visual match if you cannot find a model number.

**Why does my faucet leak only when the water is on?**
A leak that only occurs when the faucet is running usually indicates a worn O-ring on the spout body or a loose connection. A leak that occurs when the faucet is off indicates a failed internal seal or washer.

**Can I use any washer as a replacement?**
No. Washers must be the correct size and material for your specific faucet. Using the wrong washer will result in a temporary fix at best and can cause additional damage to the valve seat.

Fixing a leaky faucet is one of the most practical skills a homeowner can have. The repair is quick, the parts are inexpensive, and the satisfaction of silencing that persistent drip is genuinely rewarding.
