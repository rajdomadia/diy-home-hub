## Why Proper Technique Matters

Painting walls seems like one of the simplest home improvement tasks, yet it is also one of the most commonly done poorly. Drips, streaks, uneven coverage, and messy edges are hallmarks of rushed painting jobs. The difference between an amateur result and a professional-looking finish comes down to preparation and technique, not expensive tools or special talent.

A well-painted room can completely change the atmosphere of your home. Fresh paint makes spaces feel cleaner, larger, and more inviting. And the best part is that painting is genuinely accessible to beginners. With the right approach, your first room can look just as good as one done by a professional painter.

## Choosing the Right Paint

### Paint Finish Types

The finish you choose affects both the appearance and the practicality of your painted walls.

**Flat or Matte** finishes hide imperfections better than any other option. They create a sophisticated, velvety look but are the hardest to clean. Best for ceilings, low-traffic rooms, and formal spaces like dining rooms.

**Eggshell** offers a very slight sheen that is easier to clean than flat paint. It is the most popular choice for living rooms, bedrooms, and hallways. It hides minor wall imperfections while still being washable.

**Satin** has a noticeable soft sheen and cleans up easily. It works well for kitchens, bathrooms, kids' rooms, and high-traffic areas. Satin does show wall imperfections more than flatter finishes.

**Semi-Gloss** is highly durable and moisture-resistant. It is the standard choice for trim, doors, cabinets, and bathroom walls. The shiny finish highlights any surface imperfections, so good preparation is essential.

### How Much Paint to Buy

Measure the total wall area by multiplying the room perimeter by the wall height. Subtract the area of windows and doors. One gallon of paint covers approximately 350 to 400 square feet with one coat. Since most color changes require two coats, divide your total area by 175 to estimate the number of gallons needed.

Always buy slightly more than your calculation suggests. Having extra paint for touch-ups is much better than running out mid-wall and dealing with a color batch difference.

## Surface Preparation

This is where most people cut corners and where most painting problems originate. Thorough preparation takes time but is absolutely worth the effort.

### Clear and Protect the Room

Remove all furniture from the room if possible. If pieces must stay, move them to the center and cover them completely with drop cloths. Remove all wall hangings, outlet covers, switch plates, and light fixtures. Place painter's tape over outlets and switches.

Lay drop cloths along every wall you plan to paint. Canvas drop cloths are superior to plastic because they stay in place and absorb drips rather than letting paint pool on the surface.

### Clean the Walls

Dust all walls from top to bottom with a microfiber cloth or duster. For kitchens and bathrooms, wash the walls with a solution of warm water and trisodium phosphate (TSP) to remove grease and soap residue. Rinse with clean water and allow to dry completely.

### Repair Imperfections

Fill nail holes, dents, and small cracks with lightweight spackle. Apply the spackle with a putty knife, pressing firmly to fill the hole completely. Overfill slightly because spackle shrinks as it dries.

For larger cracks or damaged drywall, use mesh tape and joint compound. Apply in thin layers, allowing each layer to dry before adding the next. This prevents cracking and produces a smoother finish.

Once all repairs are dry, sand them smooth with 150-grit sandpaper. Feather the edges so the repair blends seamlessly into the surrounding wall. Wipe away all sanding dust with a damp cloth.

### Prime When Necessary

You need primer when painting over new drywall or spackle repairs, covering dark colors with lighter ones, painting over stains or discoloration, or switching between oil-based and water-based paints. A good primer ensures even color absorption and better adhesion for your topcoat.

## Painting Technique

### Cutting In

Cutting in means painting the edges and corners where a roller cannot reach. This includes the ceiling line, corners where walls meet, around trim, and around windows and doors.

Load a two-and-a-half-inch angled brush by dipping it about one-third of the way into the paint. Tap the brush against the inside of the can to remove excess — never wipe it across the rim, which removes too much paint.

Hold the brush like a pencil for maximum control. Start about half an inch away from the edge and slowly work toward it, letting the paint fan out to the line. Use long, smooth strokes rather than short, choppy ones.

Cut in one wall at a time, then immediately roll that wall while the cut-in paint is still wet. This technique, called maintaining a wet edge, prevents visible lines where the brushed and rolled areas meet.

### Rolling Technique

Pour paint into a roller tray and load your roller by rolling it through the paint reservoir and then back and forth on the tray's ramp to distribute paint evenly. The roller should be fully loaded but not dripping.

Start with a large W or M pattern on the wall, covering about a four-foot-square section. Then, without reloading the roller, fill in the pattern with even, parallel strokes. Finish each section with light, vertical strokes from top to bottom to smooth out the texture.

Work from top to bottom and from the cut-in edge outward. Overlap each new section into the previous one while the paint is still wet to avoid lap marks.

Maintain consistent pressure on the roller. Too much pressure squeezes out paint and creates drips and streaks. Too little pressure leaves insufficient coverage and a stippled texture.

### Second Coat

Wait for the first coat to dry completely before applying the second coat. Check the paint can for recommended dry times, which typically range from two to four hours. The second coat follows the same process: cut in first, then roll immediately after.

The second coat is where the true color develops. If you see any thin spots or minor streaks after the first coat, the second coat will cover them. Resist the temptation to apply extra-thick first coats to avoid needing a second. Thin, even coats always produce better results.

## Clean Up and Finishing Touches

Remove painter's tape while the final coat is still slightly tacky. Pull the tape at a 45-degree angle away from the painted surface for clean lines. If you wait until the paint is fully dry, it may peel away with the tape.

Inspect the finished walls under different lighting conditions. Natural daylight, overhead lighting, and lamplight all reveal different imperfections. Touch up any missed spots or thin areas with a small brush.

Reattach outlet covers, switch plates, and any other hardware. Hang curtains and wall decor once the paint has cured for at least 48 hours.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I prevent roller marks?**
Use a high-quality roller cover and maintain consistent pressure. Always finish each section with light, parallel strokes in one direction.

**Can I paint over semi-gloss without sanding?**
Technically yes, but the new paint may not adhere well. Light sanding with 150-grit paper creates a surface that new paint can grip. At minimum, use a deglosser product.

**How long before I can move furniture back?**
You can carefully return furniture after 24 hours, but the paint will not reach full hardness for about 30 days. Be gentle with painted surfaces during this curing period.

Painting interior walls is one of the highest-impact, lowest-cost improvements you can make to your home. By investing time in proper preparation and following these professional techniques, you will achieve results that look like they were done by an expert.
