## The Appeal of Handmade Soy Candles

There is something deeply satisfying about lighting a candle that you made with your own hands. The warm glow, the fragrance you personally selected, and the knowledge that every element was chosen with care makes the experience far more meaningful than lighting a store-bought candle.

Soy candles have become the preferred choice for crafters and consumers alike, and for good reasons. Soy wax is made from soybeans, a renewable resource, making it more environmentally friendly than paraffin wax derived from petroleum. Soy burns cleaner, producing less soot and fewer toxins. It also has a lower melting point, which means soy candles burn longer and release fragrance more gradually.

Candle making is also one of the most rewarding crafts for gift-giving. A beautifully poured candle with a thoughtfully chosen scent makes an impressive handmade gift that people genuinely appreciate and use.

## Understanding Soy Wax

Not all soy wax is created equal. The type you choose affects how your candles burn, how they hold fragrance, and how they look.

**Container soy wax** (such as Golden Brands 464 or EcoSoya CB-Advanced) is formulated specifically for candles poured into jars, tins, or other containers. It has a lower melting point and adheres well to container walls.

**Pillar soy wax** is harder and has a higher melting point, allowing it to stand on its own without a container. It requires different pouring temperatures and techniques.

**Blended soy wax** combines soy with small amounts of other natural waxes like coconut or beeswax to improve scent throw, appearance, or burning characteristics. These blends are excellent for beginners because they are more forgiving and often produce superior results.

For your first candles, start with a container soy wax blend. It is the easiest to work with and produces consistent, attractive results.

## Essential Supplies

**For making the candles:**
- Soy wax flakes (one pound makes approximately two 8-ounce candles)
- Pre-tabbed cotton or wood wicks sized for your container diameter
- Candle fragrance oils (choose oils specifically rated for candle use)
- Candle dye chips or liquid dye (optional)
- Heat-safe containers such as mason jars, ceramic cups, or tin cans
- Double boiler or a pouring pot inside a larger pot of water
- Thermometer (candy or infrared thermometer works well)
- Wick centering devices or chopsticks and tape
- Scale for measuring wax and fragrance
- Stirring utensil (wooden chopstick or silicone spatula)

## Selecting the Right Wick

The wick is arguably the most important element in a candle. An incorrect wick size leads to tunneling (where only the center melts), excessive smoking, or drowning (where the wax melts faster than the wick can consume it).

Wick size corresponds to the diameter of your container. As a starting guide, CD or ECO series wicks in size 6 or 8 work well for containers with a 2.5 to 3-inch diameter. Size 10 or 12 works for containers 3 to 3.5 inches in diameter.

However, every combination of wax, fragrance, and container performs differently. Plan to make test candles and adjust wick size based on the results. This testing process is a normal part of candle making, even for experienced crafters.

## Step-by-Step Candle Making

### Step 1: Prepare Your Containers

Clean your containers thoroughly and dry them completely. Any moisture in the container can cause popping, crackling, or poor adhesion of the wax to the container walls.

Secure the wick to the bottom center of each container. Most pre-tabbed wicks have a small adhesive dot on the base. Press it firmly to the center of the container bottom. If the adhesive is not strong enough, use a small dot of hot glue instead.

Thread the wick through a centering device or lay a chopstick across the top of the container and wrap the wick around it to keep it centered and upright during pouring.

### Step 2: Measure and Melt the Wax

Weigh your wax flakes using a kitchen scale. To determine how much wax you need, fill your container with water, pour the water into a measuring cup, and note the volume. You will need approximately the same weight in ounces of wax as the container holds in fluid ounces.

Place the wax in your double boiler or pouring pot. Heat slowly over medium-low heat, stirring occasionally. Monitor the temperature with your thermometer. Soy wax melts at approximately 120 to 180 degrees Fahrenheit depending on the specific product.

Heat the wax to the temperature recommended by the manufacturer, typically around 170 to 180 degrees Fahrenheit. Do not exceed 200 degrees, as overheating degrades the wax and can be a fire hazard. Never leave melting wax unattended.

### Step 3: Add Fragrance and Color

Once the wax reaches the recommended temperature, remove it from the heat source. Let it cool slightly to the fragrance addition temperature specified by your wax manufacturer, typically around 135 to 145 degrees Fahrenheit.

Add fragrance oil at a rate of 6 to 10 percent of the wax weight. For example, add 0.8 to 1 ounce of fragrance per pound of wax. Stir gently but thoroughly for a full two minutes to ensure the fragrance is completely incorporated. Insufficient stirring is a common cause of weak scent throw.

If adding color, drop in your dye chip or liquid dye at this stage and stir until fully dissolved and evenly distributed.

### Step 4: Pour the Candles

Allow the wax to cool to the pouring temperature recommended for your specific wax, usually between 120 and 140 degrees Fahrenheit. Pouring at the correct temperature is crucial for a smooth, even surface without sinkholes or rough textures.

Pour the wax slowly and steadily into your prepared containers, filling to about half an inch below the rim. Pour in a steady stream aimed at the center of the container, not directly onto the wick.

Reserve a small amount of wax for topping off. As soy wax cools, it contracts and often forms sinkholes around the wick. You will use the reserved wax to fill these in later.

### Step 5: Cool and Cure

Allow the candles to cool at room temperature for 24 hours. Do not move them, refrigerate them, or try to accelerate the cooling process. Slow, even cooling produces the best surface finish and strongest bond between the wax and container.

After 24 hours, check for sinkholes around the wick. If present, poke a few small holes in the surface near the wick with a toothpick and pour a thin layer of your reserved wax (remelted to pouring temperature) to fill the depressions.

Trim the wick to approximately one quarter inch above the wax surface.

**Curing time** is essential for optimal scent throw. Allow your candles to cure for at least one to two weeks before burning. During this time, the fragrance oil fully binds with the wax, resulting in a much stronger scent when the candle is finally lit.

## Troubleshooting Common Issues

**Tunneling (only center melts):** Your wick is too small. Increase to the next wick size for future candles. For the current candle, allow the melt pool to reach the container edges on the first burn by burning for one hour per inch of container diameter.

**Excessive smoking:** The wick is too large, or the wick needs trimming. Trim to one quarter inch before each burn. If smoking persists, use a smaller wick size.

**Rough or bumpy surface:** The pouring temperature was too high or the candle cooled too quickly. Pour at a lower temperature and ensure slow, even cooling.

**Weak scent throw:** Increase fragrance oil percentage, ensure thorough stirring, or allow longer cure time. Some fragrances are naturally stronger than others.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How long do soy candles burn?**
A well-made 8-ounce soy candle burns for approximately 40 to 50 hours. Soy wax burns about 30 percent longer than paraffin.

**Are soy candles safe around pets?**
Soy candles are generally safer than paraffin, but keep all candles out of reach of pets. Some fragrance oils can be harmful to cats and birds, so use pet-safe fragrances if you have animals in your home.

**Can I reuse candle containers?**
Yes. Once the candle has burned out, place the container in hot water to melt the remaining wax, wipe clean, and reuse for another candle or as a storage container.
