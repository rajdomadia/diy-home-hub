## The Beauty of Resin Art

Epoxy resin art has exploded in popularity, and resin coasters are one of the best entry points into this fascinating craft. Each coaster is a miniature canvas where you control the colors, patterns, and effects. The glossy, glass-like finish of cured resin turns functional drink coasters into conversation-starting pieces of art.

What makes resin coasters particularly appealing is that no two are ever identical. The way pigments interact, the organic patterns that form as resin flows, and the subtle variations in each pour create truly one-of-a-kind pieces every single time.

## Understanding Epoxy Resin

Epoxy resin consists of two components: the resin itself and a hardener. When mixed in the correct ratio, a chemical reaction occurs that transforms the liquid mixture into a hard, clear, durable solid over a period of 24 to 72 hours.

**Art-grade epoxy resin** is formulated for decorative projects. It has a longer working time (typically 30 to 45 minutes), self-levels beautifully, and cures crystal clear. Brands like ArtResin, TotalBoat, and Pro Marine are popular choices for coasters.

**UV-resistant resin** is essential for coasters. Non-UV-stabilized resin yellows over time, especially when exposed to sunlight. Always choose a product labeled as UV-resistant for items that will see regular use.

The most common mixing ratio for art-grade epoxy is 1:1 by volume — equal parts resin and hardener. Some products use different ratios, so always read the label. Incorrect ratios result in resin that remains sticky, soft, or does not cure at all.

## Materials and Supplies

**Essentials:**
- Art-grade epoxy resin and hardener (16 ounces makes approximately 6 to 8 coasters)
- Silicone coaster molds (round, square, hexagonal, or geode-shaped)
- Resin pigments (liquid dyes, mica powder, or alcohol inks)
- Graduated mixing cups
- Wooden stir sticks
- Disposable gloves (nitrile, not latex)
- Drop cloth or silicone mat for your workspace
- Heat gun or kitchen torch (for removing air bubbles)

**Optional embellishments:**
- Gold or silver leaf
- Dried flowers
- Glitter
- Crushed crystal or stone chips

**Safety equipment:**
- Nitrile gloves
- Safety glasses
- Respiratory protection (work in a well-ventilated area)
- Long-sleeved clothing you do not mind getting resin on

## Workspace Preparation

Resin requires a clean, dust-free workspace with temperatures between 70 and 80 degrees Fahrenheit. Cold temperatures slow curing and can cause cloudiness. High humidity can create a milky appearance.

Cover your work surface with a silicone mat, wax paper, or a plastic drop cloth. Resin drips will occur, and they bond permanently to unprotected surfaces.

Ensure your workspace is level. Use a bubble level on your work surface and adjust as needed. Even a slight tilt causes the resin to pool on one side, resulting in uneven coasters.

Prepare all your colors, embellishments, and tools before mixing the resin. Once mixed, you have a limited working time, and stopping to search for supplies wastes precious minutes.

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Prepare Your Molds

Inspect silicone molds for dust, debris, or residue from previous pours. Wipe the interior with a lint-free cloth. If your molds are new, wash them with warm soapy water, rinse thoroughly, and dry completely.

Place the molds on your level work surface. If making multiple coasters, arrange them within easy reach so you can work efficiently once the resin is mixed.

### Step 2: Mix the Resin

Wearing nitrile gloves, measure the resin and hardener into separate graduated mixing cups according to the manufacturer's ratio. Pour both components into a clean mixing cup.

Stir slowly and thoroughly for a minimum of three minutes, scraping the sides and bottom of the cup. Slow stirring reduces air bubbles. Rushed or vigorous stirring introduces bubbles that are difficult to remove.

Transfer the mixed resin to a second clean cup and stir for another minute. This double-cup technique ensures that unmixed resin from the cup walls does not end up in your pour, which would create soft, uncured spots.

### Step 3: Add Color

Divide your mixed resin into separate cups for each color you plan to use. Add pigments sparingly — a little goes a long way with resin colorants. Stir each color thoroughly until no streaks remain.

**Mica powders** create metallic, shimmering effects. They are opaque and produce rich, vibrant colors.

**Alcohol inks** create vivid, translucent colors that interact beautifully in the resin, creating organic cell-like patterns.

**Liquid resin dyes** produce consistent, predictable colors. They are the easiest to control and best for beginners.

### Step 4: Pour the Coasters

For a simple two-color design, pour your base color into the mold first, filling it about two-thirds full. Then pour your accent color in a line, circle, or random pattern on top of the base.

Use a stir stick to gently swirl the colors together, or leave them to interact naturally. Each approach produces different effects. Less manipulation results in more organic, natural-looking patterns. More swirling creates deliberate, controlled designs.

For a geode or agate effect, pour white as the base, then add concentrated streaks of color along one edge. Use a heat gun to gently push the colors across the surface.

If adding embellishments like gold leaf or dried flowers, place them after pouring while the resin is still liquid. Use a toothpick to position items precisely.

### Step 5: Remove Air Bubbles

Pass a heat gun or kitchen torch quickly over the surface of each coaster. Hold the heat source six to ten inches above the resin and move it constantly — never hold it in one spot. The heat causes air bubbles to rise and pop at the surface.

Check again after five minutes and repeat if new bubbles appear. Most bubbles emerge within the first 15 minutes after pouring.

### Step 6: Cover and Cure

Cover your molds with a box, plastic bin, or other dust cover. Any dust, pet hair, or debris that settles on the resin during curing will be permanently embedded in the finished piece.

Allow the coasters to cure undisturbed for the time recommended by your resin manufacturer, typically 24 to 48 hours for a full cure. The resin may feel hard to the touch after 12 hours, but it needs the full cure time to reach maximum hardness and durability.

### Step 7: Demold and Finish

Once fully cured, flex the silicone molds to release the coasters. If any edges are sharp or uneven, sand them smooth with progressively finer sandpaper (220, 400, 600-grit).

For a perfectly smooth bottom, sand the back of each coaster with 400-grit wet sandpaper on a flat surface. Apply adhesive cork or felt backing to protect furniture surfaces from scratches.

## Advanced Techniques to Try

**Petri dish effect:** Mix alcohol inks into clear resin and add a drop of white pigment. The white sinks and spreads, creating dramatic flower-like patterns that resemble petri dish cultures.

**Ocean wave effect:** Layer white and blue resin, then use a heat gun to push the white resin into wave patterns over the blue base. This creates a realistic ocean wave appearance.

**Embedded objects:** Place photos, coins, small tiles, or fabric beneath a clear resin pour to create encapsulated designs.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How long does it take for resin to fully cure?**
Most art-grade resins reach demolding hardness in 24 hours and full cure in 72 hours. Full chemical cure, where the resin reaches maximum hardness, takes about seven days.

**Can I put hot drinks on resin coasters?**
Most cured epoxy resin can withstand temperatures up to 120 degrees Fahrenheit. Hot coffee mugs are generally fine, but placing extremely hot items directly on the surface can cause marks. Using a thin cork layer on the bottom adds insulation.

**Is resin safe to work with?**
Uncured resin can cause skin irritation and the fumes can be unpleasant. Always wear gloves, work in a well-ventilated area, and avoid skin contact. Once fully cured, epoxy resin is inert and safe for everyday use.
